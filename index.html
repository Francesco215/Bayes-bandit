The Bayes Bandit

A weekend attempt to solve the k-armed bandit problem the fully Bayesian way — by tracking a posterior over how good each arm really is, and acting on the probability that an arm is best.

The k-armed bandit, in one paragraph

You walk into a room with $K$ slot machines. Each one pays out a noisy reward drawn from its own Gaussian distribution — you don't know the means, you don't know the variances, and you have a finite number of pulls. Every pull is a tradeoff: exploit the arm that has looked best so far, or explore an arm whose true quality you're still uncertain about.

The textbook answers — $\varepsilon$-greedy, UCB, gradient bandit, optimistic initialization — each compress this tradeoff into a single scalar knob. They work, but they don't quite answer the question we actually care about:

Given everything I've seen so far, what is the probability that arm $k$ is the truly best arm?

That posterior probability is the natural object. If we can track it, we can act on it directly. This post walks through the model, the math, and the resulting policy — and then puts it head-to-head with the classical baselines.

Setup: noisy arms, hidden means

Each arm $k\in\{1,\dots,K\}$ has a true expected reward $\mu_k$ and an intrinsic noise level $\sigma_k$, both unknown to us. Pulling arm $k$ produces a reward $$ r_{k,i}\mid\mu_k,\sigma_k^2 \;\sim\; \mathcal N(\mu_k,\sigma_k^2). $$ The figure below shows five arms freshly sampled from the environment used in the study. A posterior over each arm

For each arm, the Bayesian method maintains a Normal-Inverse-Gamma posterior over the unknown Gaussian parameters: $$ p(\mu_k,\sigma_k^2 \mid D_k) $$ where $D_k$ is the set of rewards observed from arm $k$.

The marginal posterior over the latent mean is Student-$t$: $$ \mu_k \mid D_k \sim t $$ This is the distribution shown below. Each blue curve represents what the model currently believes about one arm's expected reward, not just the next noisy reward from that arm.

Pull arms below and watch each posterior tighten as evidence accumulates. The true means are shown as orange bars, so you can see the posterior move from prior uncertainty toward the hidden reward landscape.

Posterior update details

The conjugate prior for an unknown-mean, unknown-variance Gaussian is the Normal-Inverse-Gamma prior: $$ \sigma_k^2 \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}(\alpha_0,\beta_0), \qquad \mu_k\mid\sigma_k^2 \sim \mathcal N\!\left(\mu_0,\tfrac{\sigma_k^2}{\kappa_0}\right). $$ The four hyperparameters $(\mu_0,\kappa_0,\alpha_0,\beta_0)$ encode what we believe before pulling anything. After observing rewards $D_k=\{r_{k,1},\dots,r_{k,n_k}\}$ with sum $s_k$ and squared-sum $q_k$, conjugacy gives us a closed-form posterior of the same family: $$ \kappa_{k,n} = \kappa_0 + n_k, \qquad \mu_{k,n} = \frac{\kappa_0\mu_0 + s_k}{\kappa_0 + n_k}, $$ $$ \alpha_{k,n} = \alpha_0 + \tfrac{n_k}{2}, \qquad \beta_{k,n} = \beta_0 + \tfrac12\!\left(q_k + \kappa_0\mu_0^2 - \kappa_{k,n}\mu_{k,n}^2\right). $$

Marginalizing $\sigma_k^2$ out of the Normal-Inverse-Gamma posterior gives the Student-$t$ posterior over the latent mean: $$ \mu_k\mid D_k \;\sim\; t_{\nu_{k,n}}\!\left(\mu_{k,n},\,\tau_{k,n}\right), \quad \nu_{k,n}=2\alpha_{k,n}, \quad \tau_{k,n}=\sqrt{\tfrac{\beta_{k,n}}{\alpha_{k,n}\kappa_{k,n}}}. $$

The right question to ask

We have a posterior on every arm's latent mean. So far so standard. The Bayesian move is to ask something the classical algorithms never explicitly compute — the posterior probability that arm $k$ is the best arm: $$ \mathbb P(k^\star = k\mid D) \;=\; \mathbb P\!\big(\mu_k\ge \mu_j\;\forall j\ne k\,\big|\,D\big). $$ Assuming the posteriors factorize across arms (they do, since each arm only learns from its own pulls), this collapses to a one-dimensional integral: $$ \mathbb P(k^\star = k\mid D) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_k(x)\!\prod_{j\ne k} F_j(x)\,dx, $$ where $f_k$ is arm $k$'s posterior density and $F_j$ are the other arms' CDFs. In words: weight every candidate value $x$ by how plausible it is for arm $k$, times the probability that every other arm is below it.

For more than a couple of arms it's faster to estimate this by Monte Carlo. The recipe is the same one Thompson wrote down in 1933:

  1. For every arm, draw a candidate latent mean $\tilde\mu_k\sim p(\mu_k\mid D_k)$.
  2. Whichever arm has the largest sample — call it the winner of this draw.
  3. The fraction of draws that arm $k$ wins is $\widehat{\mathbb P}(k^\star=k\mid D)$.

The policy: Thompson sampling, almost for free

There's a delightful coincidence in the Monte Carlo recipe: step 1 & step 2 alone are already a reasonable policy. Sample one latent mean per arm, take the winner, pull it. That's Thompson sampling, and the probability of pulling arm $k$ under this policy is exactly $\mathbb P(k^\star=k\mid D)$ — matching exploration to belief.

The implementation in the study softens this slightly with a temperature $T$: $$ \pi(k\mid D)\;=\;\frac{\exp(\tilde\mu_k/T)}{\sum_j \exp(\tilde\mu_j/T)}, \qquad \tilde\mu_k\sim p(\mu_k\mid D_k). $$ As $T\to0$ the softmax becomes an argmax and we recover plain Thompson sampling; large $T$ adds extra exploration on top. In all the sweeps below, $T$ is small.

How does it stack up?

Five algorithms, one knob each, swept across powers of two. The benchmark is average reward over the first 1000 steps in the Gaussian k-armed environment from Sutton & Barto.

$\varepsilon$-greedy

epsilon

Exploit the empirical best most of the time, explore uniformly with probability $\varepsilon$.

UCB

c

Add an optimism bonus $c\sqrt{\log t / N_k(t)}$ to each empirical mean; pull the arg-max.

Gradient bandit

alpha

Learn soft action preferences using reward advantages; sample from the softmax.

Optimistic greedy

Q0

Initialize all $Q$-values high so every arm gets pulled at least a few times.

Bayesian P(best)

kappa, alpha, beta, T

Sample $\tilde\mu_k$ from each arm's Student-$t$ posterior; act on the argmax.

UCB wins by $0.0118$. That's not nothing, but it's also not a huge gap given that UCB has decades of tuning behind it and is famously efficient at the cumulative-reward objective. The Bayesian method edges out everything else — and it does it while answering a richer question.

Why UCB still wins on this metric (and where it stops winning)

UCB chooses $$ A_t \;=\; \arg\max_k\!\left[\hat\mu_k + c\sqrt{\tfrac{\log t}{N_k(t)}}\right]. $$ It looks Bayesian-ish — uncertain arms get an optimism bonus — but it's actually an index policy designed for one specific objective: cumulative regret. It is excellent at that objective, and the sweep reflects this.

Bayesian P(best) optimizes a different thing. Two metrics worth thinking about:

  • Recommendation accuracy: after $T$ pulls, did we identify the true best arm? $\mathbf 1\{\hat k_T = k^\star\}$.
  • Simple regret: $\mu_{k^\star} - \mu_{\hat k_T}$ — how much expected reward our final recommendation leaves on the table.

Both are best-arm-identification objectives, and both are exactly what $\mathbb P(k^\star=k\mid D)$ is computing. UCB doesn't even maintain that quantity. So while UCB will beat us on the cumulative-reward leaderboard, the Bayesian method gives us a calibrated answer to the question we usually actually care about once the experiment is over: which arm should we ship?

Aleatoric vs. epistemic, falling out of the math

One more thing the NIG posterior gives us almost for free. The predictive variance of the next reward decomposes as $$ \mathrm{Var}(R_{k,n+1}\mid D_k) \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\beta_{k,n}}{\alpha_{k,n}-1}}_{\text{aleatoric}} \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{\kappa_{k,n}}\!\cdot\!\frac{\beta_{k,n}}{\alpha_{k,n}-1}}_{\text{epistemic}}. $$ The first term is irreducible reward noise — we'd see it even if we knew the arm perfectly. The second shrinks as $1/\kappa_{k,n}=1/(\kappa_0+n_k)$, i.e. with every pull. Exploration should chase the second term, not the first. A noisy arm isn't necessarily an uncertain arm. The classical baselines blur this distinction; the Bayesian one gets it for free.

Closing thoughts

The original repositoryThis whole post is a writeup of a weekend project where the math was largely AI-generated and the code was AI-written, with the human in the loop for ideas, direction, and verification. wasn't trying to invent anything new — the core idea is just Thompson sampling, going back to 1933. What the project does demonstrate is that the fully-Bayesian, posterior-over-arm-quality framing is competitive with carefully tuned UCB on the cumulative-reward sweep, and is more directly aligned with the best-arm-identification questions we usually want to answer.

More importantly, every quantity in this post had a clean closed form: posterior parameters, Student-$t$ marginals, predictive distributions, even the decomposition of uncertainty. When the model is right, the math just falls out.
