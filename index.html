What is Curiosity? Mathematically?

What is The $K$-armed bandit problem?

The $K$-armed bandit problem is perhaps one of the worse named pieces of math, However it has important applications in reinforcement learning. Let's see how it works.

You walk into a room with $K$ slot machines. Each one pays out a noisy reward drawn from its own Gaussian distribution — you don't know the means, you don't know the variances, and you have a finite number of pulls. Your job is to earn as much money (aka. "reward") as possible.

Pull an arm

Observed rewards

Every pull is a tradeoff:

  • exploit the arm that has looked best so far, at the cost of not exploring other potentially better arms
  • explore an arm whose true quality you're still uncertain about, at the cost of exploiting the best-known arm

So, how much should we exploit and how much should we explore?

We will see, that the answer to this existential question follows easly from the math once we ask ourselfes the question:

Given the information available, what is the best action I can take?

Setup: noisy arms, hidden means

Each arm $k\in\{1,\dots,K\}$ has a true expected reward $\mu_k$ and an intrinsic noise level $\sigma_k$, both unknown to us. Pulling arm $k$ produces a reward $$ r_{k,i}\mid\mu_k,\sigma_k^2 \;\sim\; \mathcal N(\mu_k,\sigma_k^2). $$ Figure 2 shows the five arms freshly sampled from the environment used in the study. The thick bar marks the latent mean $\mu_k$; the violin shows the spread of rewards you'd actually see.

Reverse-engineering the arms

We do not know directly the true mean and standard deviation of each arm, but after pulling an arm we can collect a dataset. $D_k = \{r_{k,1},\dots,r_{k,n}\}$ of observed rewards. The bigger the dataset, the better our estimate of the true mean and std— But how do we do this quantitatively?
Just as the Gaussian model gives us the likelihood $p(D_k \mid \mu_k,\sigma_k^2)$, Bayes' rule turns that evidence around into a posterior belief over the unknown parameters: $$ p(\mu_k,\sigma_k^2 \mid D_k) $$

If we focus just on the arm's hidden mean, that posterior becomes a Student-$t$ distribution: $$ \mu_k \mid D_k \sim t $$ This is the distribution shown below in Figure 3. Each colored curve represents what the model currently believes about one arm's expected reward, not just the next noisy reward from that arm.

Each time you pull an arm the available information at your disposal increases. You can then use that information to improve your estimate of the ground truth mean.

Posterior update details

Bayes' rule says that the posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior: $$ p(\mu_k,\sigma_k^2 \mid D_k) \;\propto\; p(D_k \mid \mu_k,\sigma_k^2)\,p(\mu_k,\sigma_k^2). $$ For a Gaussian reward model with unknown mean and variance, we choose the conjugate Normal-Inverse-Gamma prior: $$ \sigma_k^2 \sim \mathrm{InvGamma}(\alpha_0,\beta_0), \qquad \mu_k\mid\sigma_k^2 \sim \mathcal N\!\left(\mu_0,\tfrac{\sigma_k^2}{\kappa_0}\right). $$ The prior term contains the four hyperparameters $(\mu_0,\kappa_0,\alpha_0,\beta_0)$, which encode what we believe before pulling anything. After observing rewards $D_k=\{r_{k,1},\dots,r_{k,n_k}\}$ with sum $s_k$ and squared-sum $q_k$, conjugacy lets us multiply likelihood and prior and stay in the same family: $$ \kappa_{k,n} = \kappa_0 + n_k, \qquad \mu_{k,n} = \frac{\kappa_0\mu_0 + s_k}{\kappa_0 + n_k}, $$ $$ \alpha_{k,n} = \alpha_0 + \tfrac{n_k}{2}, \qquad \beta_{k,n} = \beta_0 + \tfrac12\!\left(q_k + \kappa_0\mu_0^2 - \kappa_{k,n}\mu_{k,n}^2\right). $$

Marginalizing $\sigma_k^2$ out of the Normal-Inverse-Gamma posterior gives the Student-$t$ posterior over the latent mean: $$ \mu_k\mid D_k \;\sim\; t_{\nu_{k,n}}\!\left(\mu_{k,n},\,\tau_{k,n}\right), \quad \nu_{k,n}=2\alpha_{k,n}, \quad \tau_{k,n}=\sqrt{\tfrac{\beta_{k,n}}{\alpha_{k,n}\kappa_{k,n}}}. $$

The right question to ask

Given everything I've seen so far, what is the probability that arm $k$ is the truly best arm?

We can translate that question into the posteriors we just built. For each arm $k$, ask how likely it is that its latent mean is larger than every other arm's estimated mean: $$ \mathbb P(k^\star = k\mid D) \;=\; \mathbb P\!\big(\mu_k\ge \mu_j\;\forall j\ne k\,\big|\,D\big). $$

The easiest way to estimate this is by Monte Carlo. There is also another way: assuming the posteriors factorize across arms (as they do), we can collapse it into a one-dimensional integral: $$ \mathbb P(k^\star = k\mid D) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_k(x)\!\prod_{j\ne k} F_j(x)\,dx, $$ where $f_k$ is arm $k$'s posterior density and $F_j$ are the other arms' CDFs. In words: weight every candidate value $x$ by how plausible it is for arm $k$, times the probability that every other arm is below it. The recipe is the same one Thompson wrote down in 1933 (algorithm 1) :

On its own this is just a one-step decision rule. Put it inside the bandit loop, where every pull adds one more observation to $D$, and it becomes an adaptive algorithm: uncertainty drives exploration early on, then the posterior concentrates and the policy naturally settles on the arms that keep looking best (algorithm 2).

def thompson_sampling($D$):

for each arm $k$:

draw $\tilde\mu_k \sim p(\mu_k\mid D_k)$

return $\arg\max_k \tilde\mu_k$

Algorithm 1

def bayes_bandit($D$):

for $t$ = $1, 2, \ldots$:

$a_t$ = thompson_sampling($D$)

pull arm $a_t$ & observe reward $r_t$

append $(a_t, r_t)$ to $D$

return $p(\mu_k \mid D)$

Algorithm 2

As you can see this algo works pretty well and it's oddly satifying to watch!

What this teaches us about curiosity

One odd thing jumps out when looking at this algorithm going: sometimes it samples points where the expected mean is lower. Why?

It comes down to uncertainty. As the algorithm samples the winning arm, its posterior distribution narrows. We become confident in its high expected value. However, the arms we haven't pulled remain wide and highly uncertain.

A wide distribution has "fat tails". Even if its mean is lower, the right tail stretches far enough that there is a stubborn, non-zero probability of drawing a value that beats the known winner. This structural optimism is what drives Thompson Sampling to occasionally explore unproven arms.

Thompson Sampling does not choose the uncertain arm because it has a separate desire to explore. It chooses it because, under the current posterior, that arm still has some probability of being the best. Each round is still greedy in a sense: the algorithm samples from its beliefs, then plays the arm that looks best in that sampled world.

The amazing thing about this approach is that it shows that curiosity is an emergent phenomenon — not something that needs to be explicitly programmed into the algorithm!

Aleatoric vs. epistemic, falling out of the math

One more thing the NIG posterior gives us almost for free. The predictive variance of the next reward decomposes as $$ \mathrm{Var}(R_{k,n+1}\mid D_k) \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\beta_{k,n}}{\alpha_{k,n}-1}}_{\text{aleatoric}} \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{\kappa_{k,n}}\!\cdot\!\frac{\beta_{k,n}}{\alpha_{k,n}-1}}_{\text{epistemic}}. $$ The first term is irreducible reward noise — we'd see it even if we knew the arm perfectly. The second shrinks as $1/\kappa_{k,n}=1/(\kappa_0+n_k)$, i.e. with every pull. Exploration should chase the second term, not the first. A noisy arm isn't necessarily an uncertain arm. The classical baselines blur this distinction; the Bayesian one gets it for free.
